Lost at Sea, 1803

Return of the Obra Dinn: a Study with Minimal Sounds

ATTENTION: This game is best played without any prior knowledge.


⚓Narrative⚓

In 1802, the merchant ship Obra Dinn set out from London for the Orient with over 200 tons of trade goods. Six months later it hadn't met its rendezvous point at the Cape of Good Hope and was declared lost at sea. Early this morning of October 14th, 1807, the Obra Dinn drifted into port at Falmouth with damaged sails and no visible crew. As insurance investigator for the East India Company's London Office, dispatch immediately to Falmouth, find means to board the ship, and prepare an assessment of damages (note 1).

The player is the insurance investigator tasked by not only the East India Company but also Henry Evans, a former crewmember of the ship, to discover what befell the people on board. Evans grants the player a logbook journal as well as a Memento Mortem pocketwatch to view the last moments of the passengers and crew. There are 60 fates to be explored. The voyage is split into ten chapters which tell a linear story in a non-linear fashion, mostly reverse chronological order, starting with "Chapter X: The End." At first, it seems like it was a regular mutiny drawn out by greed, but it is soon revealed that there were supernatural things at play. This changes the inital tone from regular, somewhat deadpan and mysterious "what happened to the ship's crew" to almost eerie and much more interesting "what actually happened on the ship?" It's harder to predict what's happening if the causes aren't exactly normal or human-made.

The logbook can be sent back to the Company and Evans without being fully complete. If too many fates are unknown, it will be considered a failure. The player will not receive anything extra beyond a note describing Evans' disappointment. If all fates that can be solved (as "Chapter VIII: Bargain" is locked for the time being) are complete, the player receives a package from Evans' companions that thanks them for their detailed log. It also includes a rotting monkey's paw for the inspector to use the pocketwatch on. The player is now able to view what happened in the "Bargin" chapter, allowing them to finally put together hidden pieces of the puzzle and discover the captain's role in everything.

⚓Joys in Confirmations⚓

At its core, Return of the Obra Dinn is an adventure that pairs exploration and observations with logical deductions. Clues can and will be both auditory and visual. Each scene starts with a ending snippet of a conversation had before a death occurs, as heard in the first scene:

From there, the player will be launched into a freeze-frame of the corpse's final moment. They can wander around in a limited manner and observe the scene. There are some closed doors that can be interacted with, but for the most part, the player is simply a ghost in the unchanging past. Some scenes are larger than others, but the ship is never fully accessible while in the past. The game opens up the ship's holds as more and more deaths are witnessed, guiding the player through the narrative without overwhelming them. As different scenes become accessible and more of the story is revealed, it simultaneously gets easier and harder to search for clues and solve fates. Many clues are not direct and are often hidden as small details of the environment.

Fates and identities are confirmed in threes to prevent guessing through the whole game (although some can be solved via the process of elimination; I will admit there were two people I had to, for lack of a better word, 'brute force' their names/fates.). When three fates are correctly solved, a version of this cutscene and audio plays:

Ernest Lindley claims that people are drawn to puzzles because of the joy and pleasure that comes from triumphing over obstacles to achieve victory. That immediate completion joy, however, is not the sole motivator. The so-called pleasurable idea of overcoming difficulties in order to achieve victory is the prominent motivation with the joy and continuation of the puzzle being secondary (note 2). When I was playing the game, I legitimately felt that joy, that pleasure, rush through me every time I would get confirmation of correct fates. It was exhilarating, especially when I wasn't expecting the cutscene or was unsure about the names/fates I had selected.

By adding in that cutscene and sound effects, it not only helps the player to understand that they're on the right track, but it also gives them a rush of pleasure in overcoming obstacles. It acts as a checkpoint that feeds both the triumph-pleasure of achieving little pieces of success and the joy of completion while also prompting them to continue solving the puzzles to the end. This association creates a psychological impact on the player which urges them to continue the puzzle even if they're not interested in the actual plot behind the demise of the ship. For me, while I was interested in what happened to the characters and the ship, I was much more focused on the solving aspect of it, not the storytelling (which is not to say that it's not an interesting story).

It took me a little under ten hours over three days to complete the game in its entirety. It took over a lot of my thoughts during those days. All I wanted to do while working was solve the mystery of this ship and the people on board. The story cannot be separated from the game's goal of filling the logbook, but it's not what I focused on when looking around the past; rather, I wanted to keep visiting past moments and see what I was missing.

For example, there was one fate left for me to solve. I knew the others were correct, but there was something about his that was wrong. His name was correct, that much I knew, but I needed to figure out what detail I overlooked. I first believed he was killed by a spike from a beast. However, as I studied the memories he was in, I realized that while he had been initally spiked in the side and was bleeding a lot, it had fallen out as he crawled behind a wall to hide from the enemies. Something else was at play. The memory where he died is difficult to view everything happening, since his body and the hallway are the only truly accessible parts of it. Yet through the small slats in the wall, a battle rages on while he cowers. By studying the battle in more detail, I saw one of the men had shot his gun at one of the enemies but missed. The bullet's gunpowder streak had gone through the wall - exactly where the man was hiding. When I realized this, I looked at his body with fresh eyes. I could see the blood spurting out of his chest in the bullet's line. I had not seen that before because of my assumption of him bleeding out due to the spike. It was such an invigorating discovery.

⚓Minimal Music Musings⚓

When the player first enters the present-day ship, there is no music. The only sound design is the splashing of the waves, the creaking of the wood, and the whistling of the wind through of what's left of the sails (and a small conversation between the inspector and the old boatman). This sets the tone and also signals to the player that they're in the present. It helps with the immersion, especially in the beginning. I enjoyed the tranquility it brought, especially compared to the rest of the game's gruesome scenes and semi-high energy background music.

After the player uses the pocketwatch on a corpse, either part A or B of the chapter's song will play after the ending conversation. This YouTube playlist breaks up the songs into each of their A and B sections. Averaging around one minute per part, it plays with enough time for the player to get a somewhat glancing look at everything before the door opens that allows them to leave the memory. If the player stays longer than the song's duration during a scene, it will eventually fade to a quiet, slow heartbeat-like noise:

When exploring a scene and focusing on the little details, many of the outside environment stimuli fades into the background - including the music or sounds. Despite this, the player still takes that initial feeling of the music ending, absorbs it into their body and mind, and creates an opinion about it. Massumi notes that the body absorbs impluses quicker than the mind can process them but also folds those feelings in with context that the mind then can register as something conscious (note 3). The music effectively sets the tone for the gameplay while the heartbeat noise allows the player to focus on observation while not being overwhelmed by the constant bombardment of the music.

The lack of music (besides the beginning minute), but not the lack of noise, helped me observe the scenes better. While I enjoy the soundtrack and find myself listening to the entire album quite often, I liked the faint thrum echoing through the scene as I spent hours upon hours trying to figure out fates and names. It allowed me to somewhat become one with the scene, in a way, and let me focus directly on it rather than getting swept up into the music. The dramatic entrance leading to the 'let me focus' sound is a nice touch, and sometimes I wish games did that more often. I do think the minimalism in the sound design is one of the things that makes this game unique.

⚓"They're at... the bottom of the sea."⚓

Unfortunately, while it is a great game, it can only be truly experienced once. Its replay value diminishes the longer the game is played and if all fates are solved, there's little to no reason to go back (unless you're using it for an assignment or watching someone else play it for the first time). After something is known about this game, it's very difficult to play it without that information haunting each choice. The thrill of unraveling the mysteries of this game is something unlike most games I have experienced before. There are plot twists awaiting behind each corpse, even if some are more subtle and not as jarring as others.

I still highly recommend it to anyone who likes logical deduction games!
